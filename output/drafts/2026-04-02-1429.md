I shipped 6 projects in the last 90 days.

One was supposed to take a weekend. It took 3 weeks and 231 commits.

**InsightClips** started as a simple idea: extract key moments from long videos using AI. Upload a video, get the highlights back.

The first version worked... sort of. I was polling AssemblyAI every few seconds to check if transcription was done. For a 2-hour video, that's a lot of unnecessary API calls.

Then I discovered webhooks exist for a reason.

Here's what I learned the hard way:

1. **Start with webhooks, not polling**
I spent hours debugging what turned out to be rate limiting issues. The fix? One afternoon switching to webhook-based transcription. Now videos process in the background and notify when ready.

2. **90 minutes isn't enough**
Users kept uploading 2-hour videos. I had to bump the limit to 120 minutes, which meant rethinking the entire processing pipeline. Sometimes the market tells you what it wants.

3. **Build the boring stuff first**
I wanted to jump straight into AI magic. Instead, I spent weeks on video upload handling, database schemas, and error states. The unsexy infrastructure work is what makes the magic possible.

But here's what surprised me: **Product Brain** (my PM interview prep tool) shipped in 66 commits and actually works. The difference? I built the data structure first, then the interface.

Meanwhile, **Support Insights Agent** emerged from a real problem at work — analyzing customer support patterns. 17 commits, minimal but functional.

The pattern I'm seeing: the projects that solve immediate problems ship faster than the ones chasing perfect solutions.

**Synopsis-Video** (AI movie trailers) is still in prototype mode because I keep adding features instead of validating the core idea.

Excited to keep building — and learning when to stop building.

## 2. Twitter (build_diary)
**Score:** 5/5 — Ready to post
**Post ID:** 4

Shipped 400+ commits across 7 projects in the last 90 days.

The most interesting challenge? Building a real-time video processing pipeline for InsightClips.

Initially, I was polling AssemblyAI every 30 seconds to check if transcription was done. Works fine for short videos, but for 2-hour podcasts? Users were waiting 5+ minutes just staring at a loading screen.

The fix: webhooks. AssemblyAI pings my server the moment transcription completes, then I immediately start generating clips and insights.

But here's what I didn't expect — webhook reliability becomes your biggest enemy. Network timeouts, retry logic, idempotency... suddenly you're debugging why some videos process in 30 seconds while others sit in limbo for hours.

I ended up building a whole fallback system: webhook as primary, polling as backup. Added proper database states, processing queues, and raised the video limit from 90 to 120 minutes because why not make life harder.

The real learning? Users don't care about your elegant architecture. They care about their video being ready when they refresh the page. Sometimes the boring solution (polling) is actually the reliable one.

Also launched Product Brain — a quiz that tells you what type of PM you are based on how you think about products. Built it in a weekend, but spent 2 weeks perfecting the gradient cards because apparently I'm that type of PM.

What's next: working on Support Insights Agent to automatically surface patterns from customer feedback. Because manually reading 500 support tickets is nobody's idea of fun.

The theme across all these projects? AI is great at the creative stuff, but the infrastructure around it — webhooks, queues, error handling — that's where you actually spend your time.
