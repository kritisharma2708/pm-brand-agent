The PM role is evolving faster than most people realize.

While everyone's debating whether PMs should code, I've been building AI tools as side projects — and it's completely changed how I think about product.

When I built InsightClips (a tool that extracts key moments from videos), I wasn't just writing PRDs. I was:
- Implementing webhook-based transcription to replace polling
- Debugging why video processing was failing at 90+ minutes
- Making real-time decisions about which AI models to use for different tasks

That's not traditional PM work. But it's becoming the new baseline.

Here's what I'm seeing:

1. **AI tools are embedding into workflows, not replacing them**
The biggest impact isn't standalone AI products. It's AI woven into existing processes — like transcription webhooks that trigger automatically, or conversational agents that pull insights from your support tickets.

2. **PMs are becoming business owners, not feature managers**
You're expected to understand competitive positioning, drive GTM strategy, and actually impact revenue. The "write PRD, hand to engineering" days are over.

3. **Building beats theorizing**
When I hit a bug in my support insights agent, I don't write a ticket. I debug it myself. That hands-on experience shapes every product decision I make differently than someone who's never shipped code.

The real shift? PMs who can build with AI understand what's actually possible vs. what's just hype.

They make better trade-off decisions. They debug problems in real-time. They don't get blocked waiting for estimates on things they can prototype in an afternoon.

It's not about becoming an engineer. It's about being fluent enough in the tools to stay ahead of the curve.

What would you build if you could ship an AI feature this weekend?

PMs used to write specs for engineers to build AI features.

Now the best PMs are building the AI features themselves.

While everyone's debating whether AI will replace PMs, I'm watching a different shift: PMs who can actually ship with AI are becoming mini-CEOs of their product areas.

They're not just writing PRDs about "integrate ChatGPT API here" — they're prototyping with Claude, building webhooks for real-time transcription, and shipping functional demos before the first engineering sprint even starts.

This changes everything about the PM role:
- You validate ideas faster (prototype in hours, not weeks)
- You understand technical constraints deeply (because you hit them yourself)  
- You can pivot instantly when user feedback comes in

The PMs I'm seeing succeed now don't just manage roadmaps — they own P&Ls, shape go-to-market, and can code up the MVP to prove their hypothesis.

It's not about replacing engineers. It's about becoming the kind of PM who can build the bridge between user needs and technical reality.

Because when you can prototype your own ideas, you stop being a feature manager and start being a product owner.
