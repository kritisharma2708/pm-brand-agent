Just shipped a major update to InsightClips — replaced polling with webhooks for video transcription.

Sounds boring, right? 

Actually, this was one of those changes that looked simple on paper but taught me way more about building with AI than I expected.

Here's what happened:

**The Problem**
InsightClips processes long-form videos (podcasts, interviews) and extracts key moments using AI. Originally, I was polling AssemblyAI every few seconds to check if transcription was done.

This worked fine... until it didn't.

For a 90-minute podcast, that's potentially 100+ API calls just sitting there asking "are you done yet?" Like a kid in the backseat on a road trip.

**The Webhook Rabbit Hole**
Switching to webhooks seemed straightforward: AssemblyAI pings my server when transcription is complete. One call instead of 100.

But here's what I learned the hard way:

1. **Webhooks are promises, not guarantees**
Had to build retry logic because sometimes the webhook just... doesn't come. Network issues, server hiccups, cosmic rays — who knows.

2. **State management gets messy fast**
Now I'm tracking: transcription started, webhook received, processing complete, extraction running. Each state needs its own error handling.

3. **Testing webhooks locally is painful**
Spent hours with ngrok tunnels and mock payloads. Pro tip: build a webhook simulator first, then integrate the real service.

**The Payoff**
231 commits later, here's what actually shipped:
- Video duration limit raised from 90 to 120 minutes
- Near-instant processing start (no more polling delays)  
- Way cleaner architecture for the extraction pipeline

But the real win? I can now process multiple videos simultaneously without hitting rate limits.

**The Lesson**
Sometimes the "boring" infrastructure work unlocks the fun product features.

Those 231 commits weren't just about webhooks — they were about building something that can actually scale when people start using it.

Curious to know: what's one "boring" technical change that ended up being way more valuable than you expected?

After building 7 different AI projects in the last few months, I'm starting to see some patterns that no single project would have taught me.

Here's what building multiple AI tools actually looks like:

**The 70/30 rule is real**
Whether it's InsightClips (video transcription), Support Insights (customer data analysis), or Product Brain (learning quizzes) — about 70% of my time goes to integration, error handling, and edge cases.

Only 30% is the "fun" AI part.

I used to think the AI was the hard part. Turns out, it's everything else.

**Start simple, then add intelligence**
My biggest mistake early on? Starting with the AI.

Now I build the basic workflow first:
- Upload → Process → Display
- Then I figure out where AI adds the most value

With Synopsis Video, I built a basic video generator first, then added AI for script writing and visual direction.

Much cleaner than trying to AI everything from day one.

**Every project needs the same 5 pieces**
1. Input validation (users will upload anything)
2. Progress tracking (AI takes time, users get anxious)
3. Error recovery (APIs fail, models hallucinate)
4. Cost monitoring (tokens add up fast)
5. Result caching (don't recompute the same thing twice)

I've now built these patterns into a template I reuse.

**Different models for different jobs**
- GPT-4: Complex reasoning (like analyzing support tickets)
- Claude: Long context tasks (processing entire video transcripts)  
- Local models: Simple classification that needs to be fast/cheap

I used to pick one model and stick with it. Now I'm model-agnostic and choose based on the specific task.

**The integration tax is higher than you think**
Adding a new API? Budget 2-3x longer than you expect.
- Webhook setup and testing
- Rate limiting and retries  
- Different response formats
- Authentication refresh flows

AssemblyAI, OpenAI, various video APIs — they all have quirks.

The AI part works great in isolation. Making it work with everything else? That's where the time goes.

Building one AI project teaches you about AI.
Building several teaches you about systems.

What's been your biggest surprise when building with AI at scale?

Most PMs think AI will replace their judgment.

I think it's going to expose how little judgment we actually had.

I've been building AI agents that analyze support tickets, extract insights from videos, and track metrics. The scary part isn't that they work well — it's that they work *exactly* like most PMs.

They spot obvious patterns. They summarize what already happened. They generate reasonable-sounding recommendations that feel right but lack conviction.

Sound familiar?

Here's the uncomfortable truth: if your PM skills can be replicated by feeding data into Claude, you weren't really doing product management. You were doing product administration.

Real product judgment isn't about spotting trends or writing better PRDs. It's about making calls with incomplete information. Knowing which metrics lie. Understanding why users say one thing but do another.

AI can't do that because AI optimizes for being right. Product management optimizes for learning what "right" even means.

The PMs who survive aren't the ones fighting AI. They're the ones using it to eliminate all the busywork so they can focus on the one thing AI can't do:

Make decisions that don't make sense yet.
