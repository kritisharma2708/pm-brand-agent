Just shipped a new version of InsightClips that handles the messy reality of YouTube content.

You know that feeling when your tool works perfectly on your test videos... then crashes spectacularly when users feed it 3-hour podcasts? Yeah, that was me three weeks ago.

The problem: InsightClips would timeout trying to transcribe anything over 90 minutes. Users would upload their favorite long-form content, wait 10 minutes, then get a cryptic SSL error.

Here's what I learned fixing it:

1. **Sometimes the right fix is knowing when to quit**
Instead of optimizing transcription for marathon videos, I just skip anything over 90 minutes now.
Sounds like giving up? It's actually better UX.
Most valuable insights come from shorter, focused content anyway.

2. **Database sessions are finicky after heavy lifting**
The transcription process was so intensive that database connections would go stale.
Spent hours debugging what turned out to be: the app trying to use dead connections after long operations.
Now every heavy operation gets fresh database sessions.

3. **Caching isn't just about speed — it's about not being wasteful**
Added smart caching so the same video never gets processed twice.
But the real win? The app now auto-loads your latest week of content on startup and immediately starts working on insights from your first video.
Open the app, and it's already useful.

The technical stuff was straightforward once I figured it out. The hard part was admitting that sometimes "working around the problem" beats "solving the problem."

Your tool doesn't need to handle every edge case perfectly. It needs to handle the common cases gracefully and fail elegantly on the weird ones.

What's one feature you thought you needed to build perfectly... but realized you could just skip instead?

After building 4 different AI projects in the past 2 months, I'm starting to see patterns that weren't obvious when I was knee-deep in each one.

The meta-insight? Building multiple AI tools teaches you things that building just one never could.

Here's what I learned across InsightClips (YouTube analysis), CompeteIQ (competitive intelligence), Snake RL (reinforcement learning game), and a classic Snake game:

**1. The 90-minute rule applies everywhere**
InsightClips taught me this the hard way — anything that takes longer than 90 minutes will timeout and crash your system.

But this pattern shows up across all projects:
- User attention spans
- API rate limits  
- Database connection timeouts
- Even your own debugging patience

Now I design everything with natural break points.

**2. Fresh sessions after heavy lifting**
This one surprised me.

In InsightClips, long-running operations would kill database connections. In Snake RL, training sessions would corrupt game state. In CompeteIQ, large data pulls would mess up subsequent queries.

The fix is always the same: reset your connections after doing heavy work.

It's like taking a breath between sets at the gym.

**3. Start simple, add intelligence later**
I built the classic Snake game first, then Snake RL with AI agents.

The classic version taught me game mechanics. The RL version taught me where AI actually adds value.

Same with InsightClips — started with basic transcription, then added smart caching and auto-loading.

You can't optimize what you haven't built yet.

**4. The tools don't matter as much as you think**
I used different stacks across all projects. Django, React, AssemblyAI, OpenAI, DQN agents.

The wins came from understanding the problem, not mastering the tools.

AssemblyAI's raw HTTP API worked better than their SDK. Manual database session management beat automatic. Simple caching beat complex ML recommendations.

**The real pattern?** 
AI projects fail at the edges — timeouts, connection drops, edge cases you didn't plan for.

The magic happens when you build systems that breathe.

What patterns have you noticed building across multiple projects?
