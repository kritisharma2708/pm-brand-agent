Just shipped a major update to InsightClips and honestly? I underestimated how hard video processing at scale would be.

InsightClips extracts key insights from YouTube videos — think of it as your personal research assistant that watches long-form content and gives you the TL;DR with actual depth.

The good news: we now support Hindi content and switched our featured creator from Think School to Raj Shamani (way better RSS feeds).

The reality check: everything broke when we hit real-world usage.

Here's what went sideways:
1. **90-minute videos killed our servers** — SSL timeouts during transcription meant users staring at loading screens forever. Solution? Skip anything over 90 minutes and actually tell people why.

2. **Database connections were a nightmare** — Heavy video processing would leave orphaned DB sessions everywhere. Spent hours debugging what turned out to be session management during long-running operations. Now each insight extraction manages its own DB lifecycle.

3. **Cache everything or suffer** — Re-processing the same video transcript multiple times? Pure waste. Added transcript caching and suddenly everything felt snappy again.

But here's what surprised me: the constraint of limiting users to 5 detailed insights actually made the product better. Forces you to surface what really matters instead of drowning people in bullet points.

Shoutout to Kriti Sharma who did the heavy lifting on the backend rebuild — the app now auto-loads the latest content and kicks off processing seamlessly.

The biggest lesson? Video processing isn't just about the AI models. It's about handling timeouts, managing resources, and designing for the reality that some content is just too long to process elegantly.

Next up: figuring out how to handle those 3-hour podcast episodes without breaking everything.

What's the longest piece of content you wish you could get insights from? Curious to know if we should tackle those marathon videos or keep it focused.

The platforms that got us here (Gmail, Google, even OpenAI) are now the gatekeepers deciding which AI tools succeed.

Here's what I've learned building AI products:

1. **Distribution risk is existential risk**
If Gmail flags your transactional emails, your onboarding breaks.
If OpenAI removes a feature you built around, your product breaks.
If Google changes their algorithm, your discovery breaks.

2. **The AI gold rush created new dependencies**
We went from "don't build on Facebook's platform" to "let's build everything on OpenAI's API."
Same mistake, shinier wrapper.

3. **Own your critical path**
Email delivery? Use multiple providers and your own domain reputation.
AI features? Have fallback models ready.
User acquisition? Build direct relationships, not just platform dependence.

The companies that survive the AI shakeout won't be the ones with the best models.
They'll be the ones who own their relationship with users.

We're so focused on training better models, we forgot the lesson every developer learned in 2018: platforms are not your friend.

After building everything from competitive analysis tools to AI-powered games over the past 8 weeks, I've noticed some patterns that you only see when you ship multiple AI projects.

Here's what building 5+ AI tools taught me that building just one never would:

**1. The "Second System" Effect is Real**
My first project (Snake Game) was clean, minimal, one commit.
By InsightClips, I had 47 commits fixing edge cases I didn't even know existed.

The learning: Start simple, then add complexity. Not the other way around.

**2. Infrastructure Debt Hits Fast**
CompeteIQ worked fine as a demo.
But when I built InsightClips to handle real YouTube videos, I spent more time fixing database timeouts than building features.

Now I plan for scale from commit #1, even if it feels like overkill.

**3. AI Models Have Personalities**
Different projects taught me that models behave differently under stress:
- Snake RL: DQN was great for game strategy but terrible at edge case handling
- InsightClips: Had to switch from one LLM to another mid-project because of language support gaps

The meta-insight? Test your AI stack with real-world messiness, not clean demo data.

**4. The 90-Minute Rule**
Learned this the hard way with InsightClips: any AI operation over 90 minutes will timeout somewhere in your stack.

Now I build with time limits first, features second.

**5. Caching Is Your Best Friend**
Across every project, the biggest performance gains came from not re-processing the same data.
Snake RL: cached game states
InsightClips: cached transcripts
CompeteIQ: cached competitor data

If your AI tool doesn't cache, you're burning money and time.

The biggest surprise? Each project taught me something that made the next one better. But only because I kept shipping, not just planning.

What patterns have you noticed across your AI projects?
