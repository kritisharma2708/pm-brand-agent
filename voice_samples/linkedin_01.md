# Platform: LinkedIn
# Type: Thought leadership / learnings post

I used to think prompting was just about writing better instructions.
Turns out, I was wrong.
While building projects with AI, I realized prompting isn't about instructions, it's about collaboration.

Here's what actually made a difference:
1. Examples > explanations
When I built VibeWatch (an app that suggests what to watch based on your mood), I included examples of the experience I wanted.

The output?
Almost exactly what I had in mind.

But in another project, I skipped the examples — and spent hours iterating on designs that kept missing the mark.

Now every prompt I write starts with context + examples.

It's the difference between giving someone a destination vs saying:
"Drive somewhere nice."

2. Use multiple LLMs — they're better as a team
I usually build on Replit or Claude Code.

But when I hit bugs, I take the problem to ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini.

Different models think differently.

What one model struggles with, another solves in seconds.

Treat them like teammates, not competitors.

3. Give clarity, not constraints
This one surprised me.

When building VibeWatch, I described the structure of the experience but didn't dictate exactly how each question should look.

The AI suggested:
- multiselect options
- slider bars
- and even added a question I hadn't thought of: "What type of content do you want to avoid?"

That's when it clicked.
Prompting isn't just giving instructions.
It's collaborating with a system that can generate ideas too.

Set the direction.
Provide clarity.
But don't box it in.

These systems can think creatively now.
They're not just following instructions — they're contributing ideas.
